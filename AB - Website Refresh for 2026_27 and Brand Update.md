# History of the Academics

### **1883** \- How it all Began

When the disciples of the Frederician Latin School became students in 1883 and moved to Copenhagen, they brought more than their academic knowledge with them. As the game of football had not yet fully taken off in Denmark, these students instead started a cricket club as a gathering point for their sporting activities.

This club was named “Fredericia Students’ Cricket Club” (FSK), as an honor to their old school. The kits were already in the well-known AB colors: green and white.

### 1887 \- Becoming Akademisk Boldklub

As early as 1887, the game of football was included in FSK. This happened after a Danish translation of the laws of the English Football Association had been made in the fall of 1886/87.

As the number of Copenhageners grew, the club’s name no longer represented its members. On February 26, 1889, the “Fredericia Students’ Cricket Club” merged with the slightly younger “Polytechnic Club”, forming the Akademisk Boldklub.

#### Entry Requirements for the Academics

After the merger, the entry requirements for membership of the newly established Akademisk Boldklub were changed so that anyone who had taken an entrance exam to university or the polytechnic school could become a member. This also applied to students from other Scandinavian universities.

In addition, non-academic footballers with potential could be admitted to AB by being unanimously proposed by the board or by 15 members of the club.

#### AB’s Initial Board of Directors

After the merger, AB’s new board consisted of E. Pagh as Chairman (former Chairman of Fredericia-Student Cricket Club), E. Faber, H. Forchhammer, H.C. Lund and H. Birk.

#### Financing the pitch

On April 1, 1889, the club moved to new grounds at Helgesensgade 5, where they were well received by the club’s first clothing sponsor. Unable to afford new towels for the club’s squad, the mother of an AB player bought fabric on credit and sewed 6 towels – which she happily presented to the excited players.

At the same time, the Danish Football Association (DBU) had purchased a fancy trophy for the tidy sum of 150 SEK and held the league’s first tournament in the 1889-1890 season. AB won the final against KB with a score of 2-0.

Around the turn of the 20th century, both KB and B93 had their own pitches and AB thought they should have their own too. The club raised approximately DKK 60,000 from the club’s supporters and were able to rent an area of 6 acres of land on Lammefælleden on Tagensvej from the City of Copenhagen. The pitch was ready for play in 1903\.

#### Defining Personalities in AB

One of the biggest personalities in AB at the time was Head of Department, Charles Buchwald, who played all three ball games available at AB: football, tennis and cricket.

Buchwald played on the national football and cricket teams and was a club tennis champion. He made his debut on AB’s 1st team in football at the age of 16, the same age that Harald Bohr made his debut, earning 4 caps.

Bohr’s brother Niels, who was probably better known off the pitch, was a goalkeeper, and the following story shows that he had a lot more on his mind than football:

_“AB was quite superior in a match against a selected German team – ‘Mittweida’ – and Niels Bohr passed the time by standing and leaning against one goal post. Suddenly a long ball was kicked towards AB’s goal. Everyone expected Niels to go out and take the ball, but he did nothing. After the match, he had to admit that his thoughts were on a mathematical problem that occupied him more than the match.“_

### **1894** \- Establishment of the Junior Department

As this new game of football had not yet reached the schools, AB worked to establish a Junior Department starting in 1894\. By 1899, the academy was finally going strong under the leadership of Edmond Andersen, who is widely credited as the creator of AB’s famous junior department and its leader for 20 years. During the chapter’s first year of existence, the number of members increased to almost 70\. Edmond Andersen kept a meticulous diary of each training session – both big and small, an example of which is below:

_Training Session Diary and the Age of the Rogue (1919)_

- _22-12: 10 men. Five-a-side. It was established for the 50th time that X is in the rogue age._
- _27-12: 20 players. Nice weather. Good game\!_
- _3.-1.-01: No players met. Frost. Skating conditions. The season ended._
- _14th-6th: 23 men in training. Harald Bohr took a plunge._

  _In 1919, the clouds began to gather over the courts on Tagensvej. The City of Copenhagen terminated the lease, but the club had it postponed several times due to many negotiations that took a very long time\! But in May 1922, it was finally over. AB was referred to Fælleden and was graciously allowed to use a piece of the grounds of Rigshospitalet. During this time, the changing rooms were in a shed without water._

* _From the Journal of Edmond Andersen_

### **1924** \- Move to Nørre Allé

The move to Nørre Allé was only made possible through great financial goodwill from well-known names within the academic circle, and not least from the football club FREM, which granted AB a large loan for the construction of new pitches and a clubhouse, which was completed on September 13, 1924\. That it had cost the club money can be seen from the fact that at the inauguration party that evening, the invitation stated the following: “sandwiches must be brought, only the spiritual food would the Committee provide”.

The year before, P.W. Degner joined the club, and from the start he took over the management of the youth department. The number of members in the youth section grew steadily – from 130 in 1925 to over 200 in the mid-30s.

AB won the junior tournament 7 times under the leadership of the renowned Carl Skomager Hansen, who was also the first Dane to turn professional abroad when he signed a deal with Glasgow Rangers in 1921 for a transfer fee of £20\!

Club life flourished in the interwar years, and the big party of the year was the ‘Rusgildet’. Here, the newly promoted seniors were introduced to the rules and customs of the club in the form of speeches, songs, a revue and, last but not least, lots of drinks\!

### **1940** \- AB’s 50th Birthday

AB’s 50th birthday was held at the hottest restaurant of the time, Nimb, where Chairman O. Thieslen welcomed the guests, and among other things, the chairman played the gramophone record that Professor and Nobel Prize winner Niels Bohr had sent from his trip to the USA, which contained a tribute to the birthday boy.

Playing ball during wartime was different than it had been before 1940\. People trained harder, enjoyed the camaraderie of the club and felt part of the community in a completely different way than before. In the 1942-43 season, AB won ‘The Double’ and the team included Knud Lundberg, the doctor who played on three national teams at the same time – football, handball and basketball, Karl Aage Hansen, later a professional in Juventus, Ivan Jensen and Georg Dahlfelt, who played with Lundberg on the national handball team.

### 1946 \- A New Sport is Added

In 1946, table tennis was added to AB. With various cricket and football players on the team, they managed to win the Copenhagen Championship in 1946 and 47, and the crowning glory was ‘The Double’ (DM and cup) in both 1948 and 1949\. Despite the great results, the department was disbanded in the early 50s.

AB’s golden age period was in the time after World War II. Membership was well over 1,000 and the flow of Danish championships increased, with victories in 1945, 1947, 1951 and 1952\. And football was popular, with 30,000 spectators in Idrætsparken for most matches.

AB had six players in the squad for the 1948 London Olympics: Knud Lundberg, Dahlfelt, Karl Aage Hansen, Ivan Jensen and the back pair Poul Petersen and Knud Bastrup-Birk. When many of these players retired – Lundberg stayed until 1960 – the results were a little more modest, despite the addition of new players: Chr. Brøgger, Dan Ohland Andersen, Frank Rechendorf, Ejler and Poul Koch and Jens Carl Christensen.

Eventually, the surroundings became too small as housing was being built on many of the former pitches, so they explored the possibility of moving to new and larger surroundings. When AB’s agreement with the Ministry of Education was terminated, they had to look for other areas. Letters were written to many municipalities, but only in Gladsaxe Municipality was the club’s request met with goodwill, and soon plans were on the table.

### 1960 \- Merger with Bagsværd

Bagsværd Idrætsforening offered to merge with AB, and when the dynamic mayor of Gladsaxe Municipality, Erhard Jacobsen, and the rest of the municipal council supported the idea, this led to the first discussions between AB’s chairman C. V. Jensen, the municipality and BIF.

The initial plan was to expand Bagsværd Stadium, but it fell through when Gladsaxe Municipality offered to buy a 12-hectare area at Skovbrynet in Bagsværd, on the condition that AB and BIF’s football departments were merged.

Long and tough negotiations between the two football clubs led in late 1961 to both associations’ general meetings agreeing to the merger – at the old Nørre Allé, however, it took three general meetings\!

### **1963** \- The Creation of AB’s Modern Home

On the beautifully situated area at Skovbrynet, 8 football pitches were laid out and a sports hall with 3 tennis courts, restaurant, changing rooms and meeting rooms was built – the largest in Northern Europe at the time\! AB Hallen was completed in 1966, but the facility was inaugurated in the fall of 1963 when the two gravel pitches were put into use.

The start of our era in Gladsaxe Municipality began just as the club had captured the Danish champions in 1967\. The team consisted of young players who had played in the club since their youth and in 1970 they won silver medals \- just one goal away from another Danish championship.

During this period, the club was constantly involved in European football, which resulted in a great overall victory over FC Zurich, as well as straight games against AEK Athens, Anderlecht and Dundee United. In 1970, AB had drawn Slima Wanderers from Malta in the Fair City tournament and won the first match at home 7-0. In the return match, on a hopeless gravel pitch, AB’s coach had ordered hesitant and cautious play in the first 15 minutes, but chairman Hans Frost intervened and suggested pressing from the start, scoring a quick goal, and then entertaining the Maltese for the rest of the match with small balls in triangles. Søren Jensen, also known as Søren Sutsko, parried orders and scored already after 3 minutes – in his own goal\! As captain Niels Yde said in his speech at the dinner: _“Søren should be praised. He followed the chairman’s advice\!”_

However, AB won the match 3-2. Many of the talented players were sold abroad (including Kresten Bjerre, Flemming Kjærsgaard, Finn Wiberg, Johnny Petersen, Aage Hansen and Benny Nielsen), and it was difficult to find qualified replacements.

### **1974 through 1985** \- A Tough Period

After the glory years of the 1960’s, things went downhill, with the club performing in both Denmark’s second and third best leagues. From 1974 to 1985, AB’s first team was consistently placed in Denmark’s second tier, despite countless opportunities for promotion. In the 1986 season, the club was relegated to the 3rd division, and for one year they were close to being relegated to the Danish league.

### **1990** \- The Era of Henriksen, Frandsen and Rasmussen

In the early 90s, things at AB started to climb again – mainly because a group of talented players who had played for the club for many years stayed on to rebuild AB, even though they were offered lucrative packages to join at other top flight clubs. These players valued the camaraderie and special spirit of AB more than the money at other clubs. Three players in particular deserve to be mentioned: René Henriksen, who at the age of 28 made his debut for the Danish national team, Peter Frandsen and Peter Rasmussen – who holds the club record in AB with 394 games – just 2 games more than Peter Frandsen.

These three greats helped AB return to the country’s best football league in 1996, where they won bronze medals in 1999 and 2000, as well as a cup triumph in 1999 with a 2-1 victory over AaB.

In 2001, AB reached its third cup final in seven years, but was beaten 1-4 by Silkeborg in Parken.

### **2004** \- The Fall from Superliga and Vision 2010

After 8 seasons in the country’s best league, AB left the top tier in 2004 and was relegated to the 1st Division. AB began to rebuild and aimed for promotion in 2010 \- rallying the supporters and players with the call to action of “_Vision 2010_”. The effort was close to succeeding, but in the end the club had to admit that the big bet did not bear fruit. This was followed by a few years where AB’s squad consisted of part-time professionals. 3 years followed where they saved their existence in the last round of matches on the finishing line. There was euphoria every time, with some great finishes in terms of points, and it seemed that AB was destined to survive.

In the 2014/15 season, however, things went wrong. After a great final spurt again, AB lost the last game and were relegated to the 2nd Division. It was a quick return to the 1.Division for the Academics, who after a mixed fall played fantastic football in the spring and won 12 out of 16 matches, scoring 38 goals. Unfortunately, it was not enough and the following season they were relegated back to the 2nd Division.

### **2022** \- Five Castles Ownership and New Approach

In November 2022, AB was sold to the British-American investor group Five Castles Football Group LLC.The consortium consisted of individual investors, most of whom were based in New York City. The former chairman of the board, Torben Oldenborg, remained in the club as a minority shareholder.

Leadership of the investment group included former New York Red Bulls’ CEO Erik Stover, football writer Ben Lyttleton, ClipperData co-founder Simon Young, former New York Cosmos CEO Jen Hsiang Chang, sports analytics consultant Andy Glockner and Dell talent manager Alex Lee. Jen Chang was appointed as Chairman as well as Sporting Director. Erik Stover became the new CEO of AB A/S.

**2023 \- 2025** New Transitions

From a sporting angle, Patrick Braune was the head coach of AB at the time of the Five Castles acquisition, with a record of 13 wins, 9 draws and 10 losses.

AB hired head coach David Roufpanah for the 2023-2024 season, who had extensive coaching experience from Allsvenskan, Superettan, the Swedish 2nd division and Damallsvenskan. AB finished that season with 9 wins, 13 draws and 10 losses.

And in November 2023, Sofie Brand Petersen replaced Stover, who decided to step down from his position as CEO. In order to give his full attention to Sporting, Chang stepped down from the Chairman position and AB A/S Board Member Brian Grieco was elected as AB A/S’s chairman, positions both men hold today.

AB parted ways with head coach David Roufpanah after 1 season. In May 2024, Joey Guðjónsson became the club’s new head coach. Guðjónsson has been an assistant coach of the Icelandic national team since 2022\. He was named Icelandic Coach of the Year in 2018\. Guðjónsson has also had an impressive playing career as a central midfielder, where he played at the highest level in England, Holland and Spain, among others. He spent 10 seasons in English football, including at Aston Villa in the Premier League and at Wolves and Burnley. He also represented the Icelandic senior national team 34 times. AB finished the 24/25 season with positive momentum, with a 4th place finish and a record of 14 wins, 9 draws and 9 losses.

**2025 \- Present** Next Stop, Promotion

In April 2025, Green Owls Football Holdings (“Green Owls”) became a minority shareholder. The investment supported the club’s mission by strengthening the team, enhancing the fan experience, expanding sponsor partnerships, and deepening community outreach, while leveraging technological and infrastructural upgrades to optimize the club.

After a bumpy start where AB suffered a loss to Helsingør in the home opener, AB finished the first half of the season firmly in first place of the league, winning 8 of the last 9 games.

In December 2025, Guðjónsson left the club for family reasons and AB hired Fannar Berg Gunnólfsson as AB’s head coach. He had previously been an assistant coach under Guðjónsson’s tenure.

In other staffing news, Henrik Bom, former head of the Danish American Chamber of Commerce in New York City, became the new AB A/S President, Head of Commercial, to spearhead AB A/S’ commercial strategy and revenue growth in February 2026, working side by side with CEO Sofie Brandi Petersen to create strategic alliances and partnerships in Denmark and abroad.

AB finished the regular season with 4 wins, 2 draws and 0 losses in the final 6 games and a promotion record of 7 wins and 3 losses. Despite a 2-1 loss to FC Roskilde in week 28, HIK beat third place Næstved, thereby ensuring AB promotion to 1.Division, with four games left in the season. And in week 30 against HIK, AB only needed a point to secure the 2.Division championship. AB delivered a smashing 5-0 win to become champions.

For the first time in 10 years, AB has returned to the 2nd tier (Betinia Liga, 1\. Division) under the stewardship of the current Five Castles consortium ownership group, a feat achieved in only 3 full seasons.
